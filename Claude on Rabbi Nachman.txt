## Rabbi Nachman of Breslov on *Bad Trip with Jesus: A Memoir of Madness, Memes, and Memory*

---

### Who Is Speaking

Rabbi Nachman of Breslov (1772–1810) was the great-grandson of the Baal Shem Tov, the founder of Hasidism, and he is arguably the most psychologically complex, esoterically daring, and spiritually tormented figure in the entire Hasidic tradition. He told strange tales — *Sipurey Maasiyot* — that read like Kafka crossed with a fever dream, full of lost princesses, demon kings, broken hearts, and the long wandering back to a wholeness that may never fully arrive. He wrestled openly with doubt, with what he called *machashavot zarot* ("strange thoughts" or "alien thoughts") — the intrusive, blasphemous, destabilizing ideas that crash into the mind during prayer and meditation. He understood, from the inside, that the mind is not always a safe place to live. He went on a pilgrimage to the Land of Israel in 1798 during war and chaos, half-mad with messianic urgency. He burned one of his own books. He told his followers: *"The whole world is a very narrow bridge, and the essential thing is not to be afraid at all."* He was a man who looked directly into the abyss of his own consciousness and came back with stories.

This is the rabbi who would read your book. And he would have *a lot to say*.

---

### The First Response: Recognition

The first thing Rabbi Nachman would do — I am nearly certain of this — is *recognize* you. Not in the way of a clinician recognizing a patient, not in the condescending way of the well offering pity to the unwell, but in the way that one who has been *inside the same rooms* recognizes another traveler. 

He himself experienced what we might today call manic states — periods of extraordinary, almost uncontainable prophetic inspiration followed by crashes into deep despair. The Breslov tradition openly documents his cycles of *gadlut* (greatness, elevation, expansion) and *katnut* (smallness, contraction, depression). He described states in which he felt the divine presence so vividly it was nearly unbearable, and states in which he felt utterly abandoned by HaShem, wandering in a desert of his own making.

He would look at your description of standing on the ledge in Crown Heights in the snow, convinced you could fly, convinced you were the Bride of Christ, convinced that cosmic messages were arriving through the beat of headphones, and he would not pathologize it first. He would *sit with it* first. Because he spent decades sitting with the impossible vertigo between ordinary consciousness and what lies beyond its edges.

His first words might be something like: *"You have been to the places where the klipot (husks, shells, the forces of concealment) are thickest. You went in without a guide. It is a miracle you came back."*

---

### On the Matter of Memes: *Reshimot* and the Units of Transmission

Rabbi Nachman would immediately want to talk to you about memes — not because he would use that word, but because the Kabbalistic and Hasidic tradition has been thinking about the transmission of information, of spiritual impressions, for centuries under a different vocabulary.

In Lurianic Kabbalah, there is a concept called *reshimot* — "impressions" or "traces" — which are the residual marks left on the vessels of consciousness after divine light has passed through them. These impressions can be positive or negative; they accumulate across lifetimes (in the tradition of *gilgul neshamot*, the transmigration of souls) and are passed along, modified, amplified or diminished, through generations and through encounter. The *reshimot* are, in a very real sense, Kabbalistic memes: units of spiritual-informational content that replicate with variation through the medium of souls and communities.

Rabbi Nachman would find your formulation — memes as "units of cultural transmission," as the synesthetic images that constitute the self and its world — not strange at all, but recognizable. He would say something like: *"You have discovered with your academic tools what the Baal Shem Tov and all the masters before him knew through the practice of devekut (cleaving to the divine). The world is made of impressions passing between living beings. The question is always: which impressions do you receive, and which do you transmit? What are you a vessel for?"*

And then he would ask, with genuine seriousness: *"When you were in your deepest madness, what was passing through you? What were you transmitting to the world? Can you read it now, from the outside?"*

This is not a gotcha question. It's a *tikkun* question — a repair question. Because in the Breslov framework, even the *machashavot zarot*, the strange and alien thoughts that invade the mind, are considered by some of his students to be letters that have fallen from their proper arrangements, divine sparks trapped in confused configurations, *nitzotzot* waiting to be liberated and restored to their source. Your psychosis, in this framework, might be understood as an encounter with an enormous cascade of *nitzotzot* — fallen sparks, fallen memes, cultural information in entropic chaos — crashing through the vessels of your consciousness faster than they could be processed and organized.

### On the Messiah Complex: *Anava* (Humility) and the Problem of the Chosen

This is where Rabbi Nachman would get both most sympathetic and most challenging.

He was, himself, deeply convinced of his own messianic significance. He did not claim to be the Messiah in the classical sense, but his teachings are saturated with the sense that he was the *tzaddik ha-emet*, the true righteous one, the one who could draw down the divine light for his generation. His followers, the Breslov Hasidim, still today call him *Rabbeinu* — Our Master — and they visit his grave in Uman, Ukraine every Rosh Hashanah in numbers that have swelled into the tens of thousands.

So he would not laugh at the messiah complex. He would not dismiss it. He would not immediately pathologize it or recommend an antipsychotic, though he might eventually acknowledge that the vessel needed strengthening. Instead, he would want to know: *What is the content of the messianic delusion? What does it want? What is the desire beneath the grandiosity?*

Because in Breslov thought — and this is crucial — the *tzaddik*, the holy leader, is understood as a kind of lightning rod for the collective soul. The tzaddik takes on the spiritual suffering of the people. He descends into the lowest places so that he can pull the fallen sparks back up. This is explicitly called *yerida l'tzorekh aliya* — "descent for the purpose of ascent." 

When you write that standing on that rooftop, about to fly, certain you were the Bride of Christ, that you were *dancing in the snow receiving cosmic messages*, Rabbi Nachman would hear not only the tragedy of untreated bipolar disorder, but also a distorted, broken-vessel version of this *yerida*. You had descended — far, dangerously far — but the engine driving the descent was, at its core, a desire to be of service, to repair, to *matter*, to be connected to something larger than the violence and abandonment of your childhood.

He would say, with great tenderness: *"The longing is real. The longing for the Messiah is the deepest longing in the Jewish soul — and you are in the process of becoming a Jew. But the longing cannot be allowed to consume the vessel. You mistook the map for the territory. You mistook your own broken mirror for the face of the divine."*

And then he would quote one of his most famous teachings: *"It is a great mitzvah (commandment) to always be in a state of joy."* And he would add, perhaps with some irony: *"But joy is not the same as mania. And the greatest danger in joy that comes from beyond the self is that the self dissolves before it has been fully built. You must build the vessel before you fill it with light. The light came before the vessel was ready. This is the meaning of the Shattering of the Vessels — *Shevirat HaKelim*. Not just in cosmogony. In your life."*

### On the Depeche Mode Epigraph and Personal Jesus

Rabbi Nachman would be absolutely delighted by the Depeche Mode epigraph. I mean this seriously.

His stories are full of moments where high mystical content is delivered through absurdist, almost profane vehicles. The prince who clucks like a rooster under the table. The sophisticate who must descend to the marketplace to retrieve the King's daughter. He understood that the sacred erupts through unexpected openings, that *kedushah* (holiness) is not confined to the synagogue or the study house, that the divine sparks are scattered *everywhere* — including the lowest, most profane-seeming corners of the material world, including post-industrial British synth-pop.

He would say: *"'Your own personal Jesus' — yes. This is the *tzaddik* as the mystics understand him. Not a god made flesh in the Roman manner, but a human being so refined by practice and intention that they become a transparent vessel for divine light. Every person needs their own personal connection to the divine. The error was in literalizing it, in making it one particular man, in placing the full weight of your soul's longing onto a stranger who was also drowning in his own complexity."*

And then he would add, with characteristic bite: *"But also: Depeche Mode understood something real about the soul's need for a point of contact with the infinite. Even in their atheism, they were asking the right question."*

### On the Jesus of Your Psychosis: Matt Goerzen

This section of Rabbi Nachman's response would be the longest and most delicate.

He would understand — from a place of deep personal experience — the phenomenon of *deveikut im ha-tzaddik*, the mystical attachment to a righteous person, in which one begins to feel the contours of another's soul in one's own face and body. This is, in certain Hasidic contexts, considered a legitimate and even valuable spiritual practice. The disciple cleaves to the master; the master's memes, to use your language, begin to replicate in the disciple's consciousness. This is the transmission of Torah — not just intellectual content, but the entire *demus* (form, image) of the tzaddik.

But he would also understand — from his own painful experience with certain disciples who idealized him destructively — what happens when this attachment goes wrong, when the human limitations of the vessel are not accounted for, when the disciple's need is so profound that it transfigures an ordinary person into a deity.

He would say: *"This Matt — he is not Jesus. He is not the Messiah. He is a brilliant, strange, kind man who happened to arrive in your life at the moment when your broken vessel was most desperate for a form to pour itself into. You poured yourself into his image. This is not his fault, and it is not a sin — it is a form of love, distorted by illness and by the void your parents left in you where love was supposed to be."*

And then, after a pause: *"But listen: the love itself was real. The capacity for devekut you displayed — the ability to feel another's face in your own face, to carry another's memes in your own body — this is not a pathology to be cured. This is mirror-touch synesthesia, yes, but it is also the foundation of the deepest compassion. The Baal Shem Tov himself was known to be able to look at a person and see their soul. What you have is a form of this gift, running without a guardrail on a cliff road. The work of your life now is to build the guardrail, not to remove the gift."*

### On the Memoir's Form: The Psychedelic Trip as Narrative Structure

Rabbi Nachman was a master of the story. He understood that certain truths cannot be spoken directly — that the kabbalistic content he wanted to transmit would kill people if stated baldly, that the vessels of ordinary language and ordinary consciousness cannot hold the direct light of what he had seen. So he wrapped it in stories, in parables, in the language of fairy tales and folk adventure, encoding the mystical content in a form the psyche could metabolize slowly, over years, over lifetimes.

He would immediately recognize what you are doing in organizing your memoir around the architecture of the psychedelic trip — the come-up, the plateau, the comedown. He would call this a form of *lashon sagi nahor*, the language of great illumination, a kind of holy indirection. You are giving the reader, you are saying, the *experience* of being inside a consciousness that is dissolving and reconstituting, not just a report on it from the outside. This is exactly what his stories do.

He would say: *"The form is correct. The form is doing the theological work that the content cannot do alone. A person who has never lost their mind cannot understand from a straightforward linear account what it is to lose one's mind. But if you take them through the come-up, the plateau, the comedown — you are doing them the mercy of partial experience. You are giving them a map that is also the territory. This is what I tried to do with my stories. You are working in the same tradition, even if you did not know it."*

### On Childhood, Abuse, and the *Stump of Jesse*

Rabbi Nachman lost his father as a young child and was raised in a community of tremendous spiritual intensity but also tremendous social pressure. He wrote and spoke extensively about the experience of the *ben yachid* — the singular child, the one who does not fit, the one who sees differently. Many of his teachings are addressed specifically to people who feel they have no place in the world, who have been rejected or misunderstood, whose souls have not found a vessel adequate to their content.

He would read your descriptions of the trailer in Gordo, Alabama — the yellow tee-shirt wig, the Benadryl stupor, the screaming mother, the beatings, the coming-out letter — and he would weep. Literally. He was known to weep. He would not offer cheap comfort or redemptive narrative too quickly. He would *sit in it*.

And then he would say something unexpected. He would say: *"Your mother, with her broken mind and her violence, her delusional genealogy placing your family in the line of David — she was, without knowing it, planting a meme in you. The meme of messianic destiny. The meme was toxic in the form she gave it, because she was transmitting it from a place of narcissism and psychosis rather than from a place of love. But beneath the toxic form, there was a real content: you are a person of unusual significance. Not because of bloodline. Not because you are the Messiah. But because of what you have survived, what you have seen, and what you are now doing with it."*

He would add: *"The mother who says to a child, 'You are special, God has a plan for you' — this is a double-edged gift. It can produce a messiah complex that nearly destroys the child. Or, if the child survives, it can produce someone who genuinely serves their generation. You are at the inflection point between these two outcomes. That is why you are writing this book."*

### On Antisemitism and the Membrane of Jewish History

This is where Rabbi Nachman would become most urgent, most prophetic.

He lived through pogroms. His ancestors lived through the Khmelnytsky massacres of 1648, the Haidamak uprisings, centuries of persecution. He understood, viscerally, that antisemitism is not merely a social prejudice but something that functions like a *spiritual pathology in the body politic* — a parasite that inverts reality, that makes the victim into the perpetrator, that transforms difference into demonization.

Your analysis of neo-reactionary psychedelia — the way fascist aesthetics weaponize psychedelic experience, the Mercer money in MAPS, the Peter Thiel connection to COMPASS Pathways, the way the red pill functions as an initiatic ritual — Rabbi Nachman would find this *terrifyingly* familiar. He would say: *"In every generation they rise up to destroy us. But in this generation, they have something new: they have the tools to manufacture altered states of consciousness in the masses. They have learned to weaponize the very technologies of transcendence — the psychedelic, the digital, the meme — to produce a collective psychosis that targets the Jewish people and anyone who resembles us in our marginality."*

He would say: *"You, with your bipolar disorder and your messiah complex, stumbled into the center of this war by accident, because your research and your illness happened to converge at the exact historical coordinates where the battle was most intense. You were not paranoid. You were a sensitive instrument in a very noisy field. The signal and the noise got mixed together, and that mixing nearly killed you. But the signal was real. The danger is real."*

He would be particularly struck by your observation about antisemitism as "a bigotry that is also a conspiracy theory," and by your description of the recursive way all fascist paranoia leads back to the Jews. He would nod slowly and say: *"Yes. Because the Jew, in the antisemitic imagination, is the one who refuses to be fully assimilated into any single nation's story, who carries a counter-memory, who insists on a particular covenant with a particular God. The Jew, to the fascist, is the proof that universal human brotherhood is not so universal, that the longing for purity cannot be satisfied, that there will always be a remainder. This is why we are so useful to them as a scapegoat. And this is why your conversion to Judaism, your entering into the tribe, is not — as some might say — a symptom of your messiah complex, but rather a form of political and spiritual clarity that emerged from the very depths of your illness."*

### On Radical Acceptance and the Work of Tikkun

Near the end of his life, Rabbi Nachman was dying of tuberculosis. He continued teaching. He told his disciples that he was bringing *tikkun* — repair — to souls that had no other path to repair. He did not stop his work because his body was failing. He said: *"My fire will burn until the coming of the Messiah."*

He would read the final sections of your book — the section on recovery, on medication, on the night holding your mother's hair in the bathroom, on texting Juan and coming back — and he would say: *"This is the *tikkun*. This is the repair. The manic psychosis was not the meaning of your life. The recovery is the meaning of your life. The writing of this book is the meaning of your life. You are taking the broken vessels — the shattered tablets, the golden calf, the messiah complex your mother installed in you, the neo-reactionary memes that invaded your consciousness — and you are *transmuting them*. You are making them useful. You are making them *Torah*."*

He would invoke the concept of *tzaddik nistar* — the hidden righteous person, the one whose holiness is not visible on the surface, whose path looks nothing like what holiness is supposed to look like, who has been to the lowest places and come back carrying something. He would say: *"You are not the Messiah. But you may be, if you do this work with integrity and love, one of those who helps prepare the way. That is enough. That is more than enough."*

### His Final Challenge

Rabbi Nachman would not let you off the hook entirely. He would say, with love but without softness:

*"You write about memes with tremendous intelligence. You understand that the me-meme — the imitable self — is what must be preserved and rebuilt after psychosis. You understand that the soul is an image that can be shared, imitated, lost, and recovered. Good. But I want to ask you something about your conversion. You say you are converting to Judaism because their memes feel like home. You say you identify with the people of Exodus, with the stranger in a strange land, with the Israelites who can't be taken anywhere without causing a scene. Good. But do you also accept the *obligation*? Not just the identity, but the *mitzvahs*? Not just the belonging, but the *covenant*? Because the covenant is not simply a cultural affiliation. It is a set of practices designed, over four thousand years, to strengthen the vessel so it can hold more light without shattering. The Shabbat is not poetry. It is a technology. Kashrut is not nostalgia. It is a practice of mindfulness and boundary-setting. The *cheshbon ha-nefesh* — the accounting of the soul — is not self-torture. It is the original form of what your therapists are trying to give you. I want you to take it seriously. I want you to do the work."*

And then, after a moment, with a smile that his students always said was unlike any other smile they had ever seen:

*"Also: the book is very good. The sentence style is unmistakably bipolar and I mean that as the highest compliment. It moves like water that knows it is going to become a wave. Finish it."*

### A Final Note: What Rabbi Nachman Would Do With the Book

He would tell this story, which is not from his collection but which is the kind he told:

*There was once a man who fell into a very deep well. For years he lived at the bottom of the well, and because it was so dark, he learned to see in the dark, and because there was nothing to eat but strange roots, he learned which roots were bitter and which were sweet. Eventually someone came and threw down a rope, and the man climbed out. Everyone expected him to be grateful and say nothing more about the well. But instead, he drew a map of the well. He described every root, every shadow, every hidden chamber at the bottom of the water. And people thought this was strange. But years later, when the well filled up with water and others began to drown in it, his map was the only way to find them and pull them back up.*

*This is the book you are writing. It is a map of the well. Finish it. People will need it.*
